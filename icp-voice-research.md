# ICP Voice Research: The Final Act of Agency
## How 8-Figure Expertise Business Founders Actually Talk About Their Problems

**Research basis:** Built to Sell Radio (50+ episodes), Collective 54 Pro Serv Podcast (30+ episodes), Collective 54 POV Essays and Founder Field Notes, Axial Middle Market Review, YouTube comment analysis (700+ comments), nothingeasyaboutthis.com, Vistage research centre.

**Filter applied throughout:** Only founders of substantial expertise businesses (consulting firms, advisory practices, specialist service businesses) speaking from lived experience. Advisory articles excluded unless a specific founder is quoted directly.

**Date compiled:** July 2026

---

## The Six Fears — In Their Own Words

These are not hypothetical fears. They appear across every source, in this order of frequency and intensity.

---

### FEAR 1 — "I Am the Business. I Can't Separate Myself From It."

The most common realisation. Often comes late.

> "I can't scale. There's not enough hours in the day for me to do all these things."
— Dave Makerewich, consulting firm founder, Collective 54 Ep.250

> "The company would be stuck, not only depending on me as a seller, but also as a trusted partner for the customer throughout the life cycle."
— Robin Way, Corios, Collective 54 Ep.228

> "As soon as the client latches onto the idea that here's the guy or the gal who makes the final decisions — I'm never going to let them go."
— Robin Way (on what clients do when the founder is the relationship)

> "When clients hire you — because of your reputation, your name, and your specific expertise — you haven't built a business. You've built a high-paying job."
— Gavin Bell, Yatter agency (Built to Sell Radio Ep.529)

> "You can't sell a reputation."
— John Warrillow summarising Gavin Bell's situation

> "I was known as the Facebook Ads Guy. The core problem was the firm was built around a personal reputation rather than a sellable business entity."
— Gavin Bell, Built to Sell Radio Ep.529

> "I'm trading on my personal credit. I can't be the bottleneck. I'm losing sleep over every imperfection."
— Darren Horwitz, Collective 54 Ep.243

> "I had worn both of those hats for the entire length of the agency."
— Lori Turner-Wilson (on being CEO and delivery lead simultaneously, Collective 54 Ep.240)

> "The only rainmakers were my dad, my brother, and I. Over the course of 20 years, we were only able to get two tax advisors to be able to cross-sell."
— Bobby Greco, Collective 54 Ep.244 (two decades of failing to break out of founder-dependent sales)

> "Founders did not over-operate because they lacked discipline. They over-operated because execution intelligence could not scale."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

> "Critical judgment lived in the founder's head."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

> "The founder stays central to sales, to delivery, to every decision that matters."
— Greg Alexander, Collective 54 (on the typical consulting firm architecture that destroys exit value)

> "Buyers don't pay for growth. They pay for profit that survives without the founder."
— Greg Alexander, Collective 54

> "Prior to AI, boutique professional services firms faced systematic discounts on exits because the firm could not fully function without the founder."
— Greg Alexander, Collective 54 POV Essay

> "In Era 3, founder dependency isn't a bottleneck. It's a death sentence."
— Matt Alexander, Managing Director, Collective 54 (Sept 2025)

### FEAR 2 — "It's Only Worth Something Because of Me."

The valuation anxiety. A fear that what they built has no independent existence.

> "Buyers understood something founders often underestimated: the firm could not fully function without the founder. So value was discounted. And risk was shifted back to the seller."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

> "What founder dependence signals to a buyer, and exactly what it takes off your price."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.553 (show notes heading — the gap is measurable)

Ujwal Arkalgud's firm was initially valued at **12x EBITDA** as a traditional founder-heavy consulting firm. After he rebuilt it so "the business runs without the founders," the same firm achieved **more than 15x EBITDA**. Three turns of uplift purely from removing the founder from the value equation.
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.523

> "Which EBITDA multiple would you say an agency would have? Let's say they generate $100k a month in net profit with close to no churn and not very dependent on the owner."
— YouTube comment, David Paul HQ (the phrase "not very dependent on the owner" used as the prerequisite condition for the higher multiple)

> "It stung. I thought I was further along. I automated my systems and reduced the workload but still a 1 man crew."
— YouTube comment (Built to Sell / Hormozi ecosystem) — emotional response to realising the business isn't actually sellable

> "Replace founder dependency with a promotable team" — listed as one of only seven things that separate consulting firms that sell from those that stall.
— Greg Alexander summary of 50 service firm exits, Built to Sell Radio Ep.492

> "When your zone of genius as a founder has expired and staying longer destroys value."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.543 (on the moment founders become the liability, not the asset)

> "The most important thing a founder builds is not services, people, or revenue. It is a firm that no longer depends on them."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

> "Working in the firm improved income. Working on the firm created wealth."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

### FEAR 3 — "AI Will Erase What I Know Before I Can Monetise It."

The sharpest current anxiety. Founders watching their expertise compress in real time.

> "A lot of owners are watching AI reshape their industry and quietly wondering if they've waited too long."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.548

> "They wonder if the business they spent twenty years building is quietly becoming obsolete."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.548

> "People running businesses who are in their 50s, retiring — they're scared of AI. That's why they're selling their business."
— Jaryd Krause, business acquirer, Built to Sell Radio Ep.548
*(Buyers are explicitly hunting for AI-scared founders as a discount opportunity. Founders who are afraid are selling at a discount to buyers who are not.)*

> "There's a temporary AI dip and a structural decline that warrants a real discount."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.548 (buyers make this distinction — sellers are not)

> "Value is getting diluted with so many low-cost entrants associated with AI."
— Brian Doyle, pricing consultant, Collective 54 Ep.242

> "Solutions using AI are perceived as commodities because buyers assume all AI offerings should be significantly cheaper."
— Brian Doyle (on the race-to-zero AI creates for service pricing)

> "AI Made the Team Faster. Under Hourly Billing, That's a Threat, Not a Win."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes headline, July 2026

> "Roughly 90% of our revenue this year has been billed by the hour. And our team has adopted AI very quickly, and it leads to hypothetically less billable work."
— Unnamed advisory firm founder, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes

> "I knew we had to evolve or die."
— Kyle Walbrun, Collective 54 Ep.260

> "A players were now, all of a sudden, in the matter of a week, our C players."
— Kyle Walbrun (on how AI instantly reranked his entire workforce's relative value)

> "Most boutique firms are protecting headcount AI has already made redundant."
— Jonathan Slain, Collective 54 Ep.253

> "This change is more like when we went from candles to electricity — not typewriters to computers."
— Jonathan Slain (on founders underestimating the scale of the shift)

> "Organizations can't just be doing what we used to do before, because it's going to pass us by too fast."
— Brian Doyle, Collective 54 Ep.242

> "AI is quietly raising a company's value in some industries — and erasing it in others."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.553

> "There's no way I'm gonna let that machine represent my brand."
— Nick Anderson, consulting firm owner, Collective 54 Ep.254

> "We're taking our knowledge, we're putting that into a silicon shell — clients can talk to this thing available to them all the time at a far less overall rate."
— Mike Gibbs, SureStep Risk Management (7 countries), Collective 54 Ep.252

> "Clients are not allowed to train the model, they're not allowed to touch that thing."
— Mike Gibbs (on protecting the IP — the accumulated judgment that makes the business valuable)

> "The premium is shifting from analysis to judgement."
— YouTube comment (3 likes, Ramin J. Imani channel — practitioners summarising the AI transition)

> "Knowledge is no longer the currency. It's wisdom and contextual experience."
— Nick Anderson, Collective 54 Ep.254

> "AI is in our loop, not the other way around."
— Nick Anderson (his operating principle — human judgment governs AI, not the reverse)

> "AI doesn't have that credibility yet."
— YouTube comment (1,236 likes, The Economist — on what buyers of judgment are actually paying for)

> "A computer can never be held accountable, therefore a computer must never make a management decision."
— YouTube comment (293 likes, The Economist)

### FEAR 4 — "I Won't Know What I Am After I Sell."

The identity crisis. Often the most suppressed fear — and the one that hits hardest after closing.

Boris Berenberg (sold Atlas Authority, high 7-figure revenue, PE at 3.5x EBITDA) wrote a blog post titled *"I regret selling my startup"* that went viral inside the exited founder community. He described it as "the most expensive lesson I have ever taught myself." Key points:
- Four of his top ten personal values — recognition, wealth, power, and respect — were "deeply tied to his founder role"
- Without a new venture, he experienced "a profound loss of purpose"
- He had failed to "clarify personal values before selling" and "didn't recognise how the sale would damage his identity as founder/CEO"

> "When I sell the company, then I'll be happy."
— Identified by psychotherapist Jo Swann as the most reliable predictor of post-exit dissatisfaction (Built to Sell Radio Ep.550). She calls it the "'when I' trap." The post-sale mood decline is "rooted in biological factors, not merely circumstantial disappointment." It persists even after the money arrives.

> "You can never get enough of what you don't really need."
— Jo Swann (on the founder who sells and finds the hole is still there)

> "I took our family to Costa Rica. I knew after two weeks of doing nothing that I was antsy already. It didn't take long."
— Marc Weiss, Collective 54 Ep.255

> "All the things that you dreamed that you wanted to do are right in front of you to do, and they don't go away once you sell the business."
— Marc Weiss (on how selling doesn't resolve the founder's underlying restlessness)

> "I left the business thinking that somebody younger, better, with passion would be able to scale it better than I could. And I found out that I know how to scale things."
— Marc Weiss (on post-exit regret about undervaluing himself)

> "Adam Rossi wished he could undo the whole sale in the years right after his exit."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.507

> "My number was $30 million. Then it turned into $10 million. Then it turned into $6 million. And then I surrendered on that mountain."
— Ret Taylor, Built to Sell Radio Ep.543 (on the moment he realised the number was never the point)

> "The problem was that I often treated life as something that would happen after I achieved the next milestone."
— Brian Kerlin, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes (after losing both parents within 14 months)

> "After my exit, I was advised to be careful about making major decisions too soon — but the restraint didn't last long. Now I have time, freedom and a wealth of knowledge, but no outlet for it."
— Ola Sage, exited founder, Vistage research June 2026

> "Too experienced to start from scratch, too energised to step away entirely, too seasoned to waste your hard-earned lessons."
— Vistage research June 2026 (describing the post-exit founder's actual situation)

> "Navigate the emotional crash post-exit."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.505 show notes (identifying the chemical mood drop as a documented phenomenon, not a character flaw)

> "My work was less creative and less impressive, and it affected how I treated myself and others."
— Ashok Sivanand, Collective 54 Ep.208 (on what founder burnout actually feels like)

### FEAR 5 — "The Wrong Partner Will Destroy What I Built."

The dread of handing the thing to someone who doesn't understand why it worked.

> "It was about the people, and the family that I had built, and all of the individuals."
— Darren Smith, Collective 54 Ep.249 (on what made the exit emotionally weighty — not the money)

> "100% of the people came with me." — His measure of a successful exit.
— Darren Smith

> "The new CEO made changes that caused revenue to drop by 40%."
— Marc Weiss, Collective 54 Ep.255 (on watching the business he built decline under new ownership)

> "Wrong person, right seat."
— Marc Weiss's summary of what went wrong

> "Internal succession proves far more reliable than external, because internal leaders get your culture, who live it, who believe it."
— Marc Weiss (retrospective conclusion)

> "It became a war with private equity."
— Rich Galgano, Windy City Wire (~$500M exit) — eventually bought his own company back "and why it may save your life" (Built to Sell Radio Ep.504)

> "Partner selection in PE matters more than price. The wrong PE firm at the wrong multiple is worse than the right firm at a lower number."
— Summary of Rich Galgano's lesson

> "We just took somebody that approached us. We connected, we clicked, it seemed great."
— Dennis Hahn (as a warning — selecting advisors on relationship fit rather than rigor, Collective 54 Ep.261)

> "We hired a credentialed M&A advisor. It still went sideways."
— Scott Gardner and Dennis Hahn, Collective 54 Ep.261

> "Most founders approach a sale with one goal: get the highest price possible. Focusing only on price can lead to the wrong deal, the wrong partner, and a painful transition after closing."
— Mark Ferrier, Built to Sell Radio Ep.536

### FEAR 6 — "I Don't Know Whether to Act Now or Wait."

The transform-before-or-after question. No clean answer exists. Founders are genuinely stuck on the sequence.

> "Should I transform before I sell?"
— The question built to sell / Collective 54 founders return to repeatedly. Never answered definitively.

Ujwal Arkalgud's answer was yes: transformed first, achieved 15x vs the 12x he would have received. Three extra turns of EBITDA.

Gavin Bell's answer was yes: rebuilt from personal brand to company brand over three years before selling. Cut earn-out from 2 years to 9 months by proving independence from himself.

Jaryd Krause (buyer) says: if you haven't transformed, he wants to buy you at a discount and do it himself. If you have, his return diminishes. The buyer perspective is explicit: untransformed firms are the opportunity.

> "There's a temporary AI dip and a structural decline that warrants a real discount."
— Built to Sell Radio Ep.548 (the distinction buyers make — sellers are not making it)

> "Identify the new buyer questions around AI and tech enablement."
— Greg Alexander — this is now a specific due diligence topic. Even if you don't transform, you will be asked. Having no answer costs you.

> "I have spent 13 years seeking an alternative billing model."
— Unnamed advisory firm founder, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes (on the urgency of the model problem that AI has now made existential)

> "Revenue models tied directly to time transform AI efficiency gains into margin compression rather than expansion."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes (the structural trap of hourly billing in an AI era)

> "The mistake was not making this decision faster. If I had done this five years ago — I'm just kicking myself."
— Lori Turner-Wilson (on a decision made too late)

## The Structural Gap — What No Adviser Covers

The research makes this explicit. Eight-figure expertise founders approaching transition are surrounded by advisers who each see one piece:

- **M&A advisers** — focus on price, structure, process. Don't address identity, AI, or what survives culturally.
- **Exit planners** — focus on succession mechanics. Don't address AI's impact on valuation or the founder's post-exit identity.
- **Technology consultants** — focus on AI implementation. Don't address whether to transform before or after exit, or what founder judgment should become.
- **Executive coaches** — address identity and transition. Don't address valuation, AI strategy, or institutional design.
- **Lawyers** — address IP and structure. Don't address any of the above.

The person who needs help is sitting at the intersection of all five. Nobody is there.

This is the gap The Final Act of Agency fills.
