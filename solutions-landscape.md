# The Solutions Landscape: What Exists for 8-Figure Expertise Business Founders
## A competitive map for The Final Act of Agency

**Research conducted:** July 2026
**Sources:** Direct site research across 30+ offerings across exit planning, AI transformation, identity transition, peer communities, M&A advisory, knowledge management platforms

---

## The Core Finding

The market for 8-figure founder-owners of expertise businesses navigating transition is served by four fragmented categories. Each covers one piece. Nobody covers all five.

The five dimensions a founder in this situation needs:

1. **Succession and exit strategy** — what is the business worth, how do I structure the transition, when and how do I sell or hand over
2. **AI strategy** — how does AI change what I should do before and after exit, what of my knowledge should become agentic, what are the implications for valuation
3. **Institutional knowledge transfer** — how do I extract, encode and transfer the accumulated judgment that took 20 years to build
4. **Identity and psychological transition** — who am I if I am not my firm, what comes next, how do I avoid the post-exit identity collapse
5. **Legacy design** — what of me should survive in the business, what should remain personal, how do I ensure the thing I built doesn't dissolve

**No single solution addresses all five. Not one.**

## Category 1: Exit Planning and M&A Advisory

### What exists

**Collective 54** (Greg Alexander) — the closest fit to the ICP. A peer mastermind community for boutique professional services firm founders at $5M–$100M. Explicitly includes a growth, scale, and exit track. Has rebranded hard toward AI-native professional services. Cost: ~$24K/year.

*What it does not address:* Peer learning only — no individual advisory, no deliverables, no structured IP extraction. US/Canada only. Does not address the founder's identity or psychological transition. No mechanism for connecting exit strategy to AI transformation.

**Value Builder / Built to Sell** (John Warrillow) — the canonical intellectual framework. Book, podcast, online assessments (Value Builder Report, PREScore, Freedom Score). The B2B2C model: Warrillow trains advisors who deliver to owners.

*What it does not address:* The entire methodology treats founder-dependency as a defect to eliminate via SOPs. For expertise businesses where the founder's judgment IS the product, this premise is either impossible or requires a fundamentally different approach. No IP extraction framework. No AI dimension. No identity support.

**FP Transitions** — the gold standard example of what vertical depth looks like. Deep RIA/wealth management specialisation, 25 years, 17,000+ clients, real M&A execution. Valuation methodology accounts for recurring revenue and human capital.

*Critical note:* Exclusively for regulated financial advisory firms. Does not transfer to consulting, leadership, or other expertise businesses. But it proves the model: deep vertical specialisation + transaction capability + multi-decade data = the offering that works. Nothing equivalent exists for the broader expertise business universe.

**Exit Planning Institute / CEPA ecosystem** — 8,000+ certified advisors. The credential is held by generalists (financial planners, accountants). The EPI's customer is the advisor, not the founder. Generic framework. Quality entirely dependent on which individual CEPA the founder happens to hire.

**Axial** — a marketplace connecting sellers to buyers and M&A advisors. A platform, not an advisor. Assumes a sellable asset already exists. No pre-sale preparation.

**M&A advisors generally** (Oaklyn, Generational Group, business brokers) — transactional. Engage when the owner is ready to sell. No pre-sale value creation, no IP structuring, no identity support. Standard fees: retainer + 1-5% of transaction value.

### The consistent gap

Every exit planning solution treats the sale as the objective and founder-dependency as a risk to eliminate. None address what the founder's judgment is actually worth, how to extract and transfer it in a way that increases value, or what the founder does with their identity on the other side.

## Category 2: AI Transformation for Professional Services

### What exists

**Consulting Success AI Masterclass** — $195/month. Monthly webinars on AI tools for individual consultants. Covers ChatGPT for proposal writing, content, research. No firm-level strategy, no exit implications, no IP extraction.

**Collective 54 AI content** — POV essays (The AI Founder, The AI Operations Manager, etc.) and podcast episodes. Rich content. Peer community accountability. Not expert advisory delivery. No structured transformation program.

**Knowledge management platforms** (Guru, Tettra, Notion AI, Otter.ai) — all share the same structural flaw: they manage knowledge that has already been documented. None solve the actual problem: extracting tacit founder judgment that has never been written down.

**Delphi.ai** — $79–$299/month. Trains an AI on uploaded content and creates an interactive Q&A in the expert's voice. Matthew Hussey case study: 2.5M conversations. Closest to "digital expert" concept but designed for public creator-to-audience dynamics, not private firm-internal knowledge transfer or exit valuation.

**MindStudio, Copy.ai, etc.** — infrastructure for building AI agents. No advisory layer, no professional services methodology, no knowledge extraction process.

### The consistent gap

The intersection of AI transformation + knowledge institutionalisation + exit implications for expertise businesses is an empty market. The six unanswered questions nobody is addressing:

1. Does AI transformation before exit increase or decrease sale price for an advisory firm — and for whom?
2. Should a founder transform then sell, or sell and let the acquirer transform?
3. How do you extract tacit founder judgment — 20 years of pattern recognition — into a structured system that can be operated and transferred?
4. How does an AI-enabled firm make the case to buyers that it commands a higher multiple?
5. What is the difference in approach between a judgment-intensive expertise business (leadership advisory) vs a process business (IT consulting)?
6. Who owns a digital version of the founder's thinking?

Not one of these questions has a packaged answer available in the market.

## Category 3: Identity, Transition, and Post-Exit Coaching

### What exists

**Strategic Coach** (Dan Sullivan) — ~$15–25K/year. Quarterly workshops, Unique Ability framework, built for ongoing active founders. The philosophy: identify what only you can do and delegate everything else. Addresses autonomy, not transition. Assumes the founder keeps going.

*What it does not address:* The exit or succession question is implicit, not the core frame. No post-exit identity work. No AI dimension.

**Tiger 21** — $20M+ net worth minimum. ~$30–50K/year. Monthly peer groups, wealth preservation focus, philanthropy. The most premium community for post-exit founders.

*What it does not address:* Wealth management, not identity or meaning. The founder who sold for £15M and now has "time, freedom, and a wealth of knowledge but no outlet for it" is not served by Tiger 21. The $20M floor also excludes many 8-figure founders.

**Vistage, YPO, EO** — peer communities for active leaders. Quality Chair/facilitator dependent. No structured transition programming. Not built for the founder stepping back.

**Jo Swann** — UK psychotherapist specifically for exited founders. Referenced in Built to Sell ecosystem as rare and genuinely relevant. Current site is down — appears to be small-scale private practice, not a scalable program.

**Beyond the Exit** (John Rood) — post-exit identity coaching. Podcast-based. Website appears inactive.

**Built to Sell PREScore** — the only tool found that explicitly assesses psychological readiness to exit. Assessment only. No follow-through, no program, no support.

### The consistent gap

The AI strategy blind spot is total — not one transition coaching or peer community program has integrated AI strategy into founder transition advice as of July 2026.

Identity transition has no premium home. Jo Swann and John Rood are the only practitioners in the space — both appear small-scale and one may be inactive.

The "who am I next?" question is unaddressed by any premium structured offering.

## What The Final Act of Agency Is — By Elimination

Every existing solution covers one corner of the problem. The founder trying to navigate this moment ends up with:

- An M&A advisor who understands deals but not AI or identity
- A peer community that provides belonging but not answers
- A CEPA-certified advisor who applies a generic framework to a non-generic business
- An AI consultant who understands tools but not succession or firm economics
- A coach or therapist who addresses the personal side but not the strategic decisions

None of these talk to each other. The founder synthesises as best they can.

The Final Act of Agency is the offering that holds all five dimensions simultaneously:

1. What is your business actually worth — and what specifically reduces that number
2. How does AI change what you should do before you make any irreversible decision
3. What of your judgment can be extracted, encoded, and transferred — and what can't
4. What does the transition do to your identity — and how do you architect what comes next
5. What of you should survive in the business — and how do you ensure that happens

Not as five separate engagements with five separate advisors.

As one coherent advisory process with one person who holds the whole picture.

That person does not currently exist in a packaged, accessible form for 8-figure expertise business founders in the UK and European market.

## Strategic Implications for Positioning

**The reference model:** FP Transitions. They built deep vertical expertise in one specific type of expertise business (regulated financial advisory) and became the definitive resource for valuation, succession, and M&A in that vertical over 25 years. The model works. Nothing equivalent exists for consulting firms, leadership advisory practices, or knowledge-intensive boutiques — particularly outside the US.

**The price benchmark:** Collective 54 charges $24K/year for peer learning with no individual advisory. Strategic Coach charges $15–25K/year for quarterly workshops. Tiger 21 charges $30–50K/year for peer groups with no coaching. The market is demonstrably willing to pay $15–50K/year for partial solutions. A comprehensive advisory offering that integrates what none of them do individually should price above this range.

**The differentiation claim that cannot be challenged:** No competitor holds all five dimensions. Any founder who has already tried one of the existing solutions — and most have — has experienced the fragmentation directly. The positioning is not "we are better than X." It is "we are the only one who holds the whole picture."

**The AI angle is currently unoccupied.** Every existing solution was built before AI became the defining variable for this founder's decision-making. Not one program in the transition/succession space has integrated AI strategy. This is not a permanent gap — it will close. But as of July 2026, it is wide open.
