# Research: How 8-Figure Founders of Expertise Businesses Talk About Their Problems

**Sources:** Built to Sell Radio, Collective 54 Pro Serv Podcast, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes and POV Essays, ThinkAdvisor, nothingeasyaboutthis.com, Mixergy
**Compiled:** July 2026
**Filter applied:** Only founders of substantial businesses speaking from lived experience. Advisory articles and strategy pieces excluded unless a specific founder is quoted.

---

## THEME 1: "I Am the Business" — Recognising the Trap

The most raw admissions happen when founders describe the moment they understood the business only existed because they existed.

**1.** "There's a lot of founder magic that makes the firm work."
— Dave Makerewich, consulting firm founder, Collective 54 Pod ep.250

**2.** "I can't scale. There's not enough hours in the day for me to do all these things."
— Dave Makerewich, ep.250 (on why the firm had hit a ceiling)

**3.** "The fastest way to make a service company unsellable is building it around a personal brand. When clients hire *you* — because of your reputation, your name, and your specific expertise — you haven't built a business; you've built a high-paying job."
— Characterisation of Gavin Bell's situation, Built to Sell Radio ep.529 (Bell confirmed)

**4.** "You can't sell a reputation."
— Built to Sell Radio ep.529, on why Gavin Bell's agency (he was "the Facebook Ads Guy" in the UK) had no buyer value before he rebuilt it

**5.** "Buyers understood something founders often underestimated: the firm could not fully function without the founder. So value was discounted. And risk was shifted back to the seller."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder" (describing Era 2 consulting firms, i.e. the standard pre-AI boutique)

**6.** "Founders did not over-operate because they lacked discipline. They over-operated because execution intelligence could not scale."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

**7.** "Critical judgment lived in the founder's head."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder" (on what kept service firms founder-dependent for two decades)

**8.** "In Era 3, founder dependency isn't a bottleneck. It's a death sentence."
— Matt Alexander, Managing Director, Collective 54 blog post (Sept 2025)

**9.** "Founders stuck in delivery instead of selling, roles that are not sharp, a pipeline that lands in lumps, all pull the number down at the same time."
— Unnamed boutique firm founder, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes (on why utilisation sat at 40% when the benchmark is 65%)

**10.** "Roughly 90% of our revenue this year, at least, has been billed by the hour. And our team has adopted AI very quickly, and it leads to hypothetically less billable work."
— Unnamed advisory firm founder, Collective 54 Founder Field Notes (on the paradox of AI making their hourly billing model self-defeating)

**11.** Dr. Michael Filosi, dental practice owner, described his personal billings representing **43% of total revenue** at the point he decided to exit — the most common form of founder dependency in service businesses, where the owner is literally the product.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.527 (Filosi feathered back from four days on the tools to zero before the sale, ultimately collecting 100% of proceeds at closing — described as "almost unheard of in dentistry")

## THEME 2: What Buyers See (and Discount)

Direct market evidence of what founder dependence costs — from buyers and M&A advisors on record.

**12.** "What founder dependence signals to a buyer, and exactly what it takes off your price."
— Episode learning bullet, Built to Sell Radio ep.553 (featuring PE advisor Lee McCabe and agency acquirer Jason Swenk)

**13.** "AI is quietly raising a company's value in some industries — and erasing it in others."
— Episode learning bullet, Built to Sell Radio ep.553

**14.** "The industries where AI quietly raises a company's value instead of erasing it" / "Why a 22-year-old armed with AI still can't replicate 30 years of brand equity overnight."
— Built to Sell Radio ep.553 (two competing bullets in the same episode, showing the ambiguity buyers themselves feel)

**15.** "When your 'zone of genius' as a founder has expired and staying longer destroys value."
— Episode learning bullet on Ret Taylor's story, Built to Sell Radio ep.543

**16.** Ujwal Arkalgud's firm was initially valued at **12x EBITDA** as a traditional founder-heavy consulting firm. After he rebuilt it so "the business runs without the founders," the same firm achieved **more than 15x EBITDA** — roughly three turns of uplift purely from removing the founder from the value equation.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.523

**17.** "Replace founder dependency with a promotable team" — listed as one of only seven things that separate consulting firms that sell from those that stall, in Greg Alexander's summary of 50 service firm exits.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.492 (Alexander sold SBI, his own firm, for $162M — 30 people)

**18.** "Identify the new buyer questions around AI and tech enablement" — listed alongside founder dependency as a top buyer concern in due diligence.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.492

**19.** "People running businesses who are 20 years old, that are retiring, they're scared of AI. That's why they're selling their business. But for me, I'm like, that's perfect."
— Jaryd Krause, business acquirer, Built to Sell Radio ep.548 (buyers are explicitly hunting for founders who are afraid of AI — they see it as a discount opportunity)

**20.** "There's a temporary AI dip and a structural decline that warrants a real discount."
— Built to Sell Radio ep.548 (framing for buyers — sellers are not making this distinction themselves)

## THEME 3: The Identity Crisis and What Gets Lost

Founders describing what they actually feared losing — not the money, but something harder to name.

**21.** Boris Berenberg (sold Atlas Authority, high 7-figure revenue, 15 employees, to PE at 3.5x EBITDA) wrote a blog post titled "I regret selling my startup" that went viral inside the exited founder community. His summary: "the most expensive lesson I have ever taught myself."
Key findings from the post:
- Four of his top ten personal values — recognition, wealth, power, and respect — were "deeply tied to his founder role"
- Without a new venture, he experienced "a profound loss of purpose"
- He had failed to "clarify personal values before selling" and "didn't recognise how the sale would damage his identity as founder/CEO"
- He underestimated his preference for "playing offense" (growth) over "playing defense" (wealth preservation)
— nothingeasyaboutthis.com (Jan 2024)

**22.** Marc Weiss (sold his consulting firm, then watched the new CEO destroy 40% of revenue by changing the strategy) said: "I didn't need the validation that the business gave me to be who I am" — but then described missing "being there to contribute and be a part of something."
— Collective 54 Pro Serv Pod ep.255 (the distinction he drew — he didn't need validation, but he needed contribution — is the real thing founders lose)

**23.** Adam Rossi "wished he could undo the whole sale in the years right after his exit." The hard part wasn't selling — it was "figuring out what to do after the deal closed."
— Built to Sell Radio ep.507

**24.** Dan Berger (Social Tables, $100M exit to Cvent) built what he called "a soft landing by building belonging outside your company" before the sale — specifically because he knew the emotional crash that follows a major exit was real and he wanted something to absorb it.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.508

**25.** "The most reliable predictor of a miserable exit" is the phrase: "When I sell the company, then I'll be happy."
Psychotherapist Jo Swann (who works with exited founders) calls this the "'when I' trap" — and notes the post-sale mood decline is "rooted in biological factors, not merely circumstantial disappointment." In other words: it persists even after the money arrives.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.550

**26.** Ret Taylor (Ned, natural remedies company, $30M target → fire sale): "My number was $30 million. Then it turned into $10 million. Then it turned into $6 million. And then I surrendered on that mountain."
He described a realisation at 18,000 feet on Denali with two arctic storms closing in: "the number was never the point." He now guides founders through life transitions on multi-day vision quests in Colorado.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.543 / ep.553

**27.** Brian Kerlin (Collective 54 member, essay July 2026) described losing both parents within 14 months and realising: "The problem was that I often treated life as something that would happen after I achieved the next milestone."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes

**28.** Ola Sage (exited founder, became Vistage Chair): "After my exit, I was advised to be careful about making major decisions too soon" — but admitted the restraint didn't last long. Now she describes having "time, freedom and a wealth of knowledge, but no outlet for it."
— Vistage research, June 2026

## THEME 4: AI Is Changing What Expertise Is Worth

These are the sharpest current conversations — founders grappling with whether AI commoditises the thing they spent 20 years building.

**29.** "Knowledge is no longer the currency. It's wisdom and contextual experience."
— Nick Anderson, consulting firm owner, Collective 54 Pod ep.254 (his reframe when he decided AI wasn't going to replace his firm)

**30.** "AI is in our loop, not the other way around."
— Nick Anderson, ep.254 (his operating principle — human judgment governs the AI, not the reverse)

**31.** "There's no way I'm gonna let that machine represent my brand."
— Nick Anderson, ep.254 (on rejecting an AI voice agent — the exact fear about identity being diluted by automation)

**32.** "We're taking our knowledge...we're putting that into a silicon shell...you can talk to this guy...available to you all the time at a far less overall rate."
— Mike Gibbs (SureStep Risk Management, GRC consulting, 7 countries), Collective 54 Pod ep.252 (on converting consultant knowledge into an always-on AI that clients can access at SaaS pricing)

**33.** Gibbs' core fear about this model: "clients are not allowed to train the model, they're not allowed to touch that thing" — he runs everything on private infrastructure specifically to prevent his firm's IP from leaking into public models or competitors' training data.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.252

**34.** "A tidal wave that's coming" — "either we're gonna surf this wave and ride it out, or you're gonna drown."
— Mike Gibbs, ep.252 (how he described the AI moment to his team)

**35.** Kyle Walbrun (EfficientAide, executive support firm): "Old model, human labor focus, task focus. Now clients want to delegate outcomes, they don't want to delegate tasks."
The firm moved from charging for administrative tasks to charging for "leadership leverage" — "ensuring only truly urgent matters reach the executive's attention."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.260

**36.** Walbrun on the cost of transformation: "we lost clients as a result of this...but we also retained a lot more."
He made a hard cutover in January — all new clients got the human+AI model, legacy clients were migrated one by one through personal conversations.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.260

**37.** "When AI flattens the work, the firm that feels different wins."
— John Roberson, consulting firm owner, Collective 54 Pod ep.258 (his answer to commoditisation: experience design — how clients *feel* during an engagement)

**38.** "The intangibles, not the deliverable, decide most close-vs-lose proposals."
— John Roberson, ep.258

**39.** "AI Made the Team Faster. Under Hourly Billing, That's a Threat, Not a Win."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes headline, July 2026 (an unnamed advisory firm founder describing the paradox directly)

**40.** "We run all that stuff internally...clients are not allowed to train the model, they're not allowed to touch that thing."
— Mike Gibbs, ep.252 (on protecting the IP that is their expertise — the thing that makes the business valuable is the accumulated judgment, and he treats it like a trade secret)

**41.** Jonathan Slain (consulting firm, Collective 54): "The firms that restructure now capture margin their competitors are still leaking." On what happens if you don't act: "we'll just keep using candles" while competitors electrify.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.253

**42.** Chris Prinos (AI implementation firm): "I always thought, well, no one's gonna be able to write an SOW that's as compelling as me with the right tone, and that's just wrong."
The precise moment of recognising that founder exceptionalism in delivery was an illusion — and the resistance was just attachment.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.262

## THEME 5: What They Wished They'd Known / Done Differently

The most useful category — honest post-mortem language from founders who had already gone through it.

**43.** Boris Berenberg (Atlas Authority exit): "I should have had more serious buyers in that third bucket of giving real offers so that I could have played them against each other a little bit."
The blog post's bigger regret: he had "no clarity on personal values before selling" and should have done "therapy and values clarification as essential pre-sale exercises, not to solve problems but to ensure informed decision-making."
— nothingeasyaboutthis.com / Built to Sell Radio ep.544

**44.** Brad McAllister (professional services firm sold to strategic buyer): "know your number, you know, run your Monte Carlo assessments, figure out what you... the lifestyle you want, what you want to leave."
His lesson: "if I'm leaving money on the table, who...who cares? Because...you know, my...I'm hitting my outcome."
He was more interested in building than selling — "I was more interested in building something lasting and really cool" — and had to be convinced by his co-founder.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.251

**45.** Darren Smith (16-year consulting firm, sold to strategic): "have the conversations... Understand what your goals are to get out of it" — because "post-sale realities differ significantly from founder expectations."
His biggest anxiety: not the price — "the amount of... angst that it was going to create across the family that I had built."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.249

**46.** Scott Gardner and Dennis Hahn (sold Liquid, M&A process went sideways): "we didn't run a competitive process, we didn't really benchmark them against alternatives" when selecting the M&A advisor.
Their advisors were "very informal" and "they would tell us to do things. It's like, go do this" — without detailed guidance.
Scott's summary: "I think it was more the time than the money, because we still performed okay" — but "you can't get back" wasted time.
Their biggest structural mistake: the advisors recommended a holding company that Scott spent "years trying to find the right partners" for, running "the Holdco more than I was running Liquid."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.261

**47.** Dennis Hahn: "we just took somebody that approached us. We connected, we clicked, it seemed great" — as a warning about selecting advisors on relationship fit rather than rigor.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.261

**48.** Marc Weiss (sold consulting firm, watched it decline under new ownership): The CEO his buyer installed made changes that caused revenue to "drop by 40%." The core failure: "wrong person, right seat."
His conclusion: internal succession proves far more reliable than external, because internal leaders "get your culture, who live it, who believe it."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.255

**49.** Rich Galgano (Windy City Wire, ~$500M exit after PE battles): describes the experience as a "war with private equity" and eventually bought his own company back — "and why it may save your life."
The lesson: partner selection in PE matters more than price. The wrong PE firm at the wrong multiple is worse than the right firm at a lower number.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.504

**50.** Jason Patel (struggling business, eventually sold for asset value): "He assumed he had zero leverage." But buyers saw value in SEO rankings, blog traffic, and reputation — assets that could be "stripped away from the expensive service delivery and plugged into their own portfolio."
The businesses owners think are worthless sometimes have embedded assets buyers want — and the parts can be worth more than the whole.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.532

## THEME 6: What "Survives" When the Founder Steps Back

The specific question of institutional survival — what the firm actually is once the founder isn't holding it together.

**51.** Peter Kang (Barrel agency, 18-year owner-operator): the transition from operator to owner took "a year and a half to two-year process" involving identifying an internal successor and getting them "up to speed on the biz dev side, the finance side, and all the aspects that a CEO needed to know."
His philosophy: "we're just shareholders in this business, but now we're getting cash flows from this business."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.263

**52.** Ronan Berder (Wiredcraft, 160 people, sold to Publicis Groupe for €67M): built "defensible margins through documented processes rather than founder heroics" and documented company culture specifically to reduce founder dependence.
The lesson from his story: the firm that survives is the one that has documented not just what it does but *how it thinks*.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.511

**53.** The digital experience consultancy founder (Collective 54) described his firm's core problem: "land a great project, finish it, and without guaranteed follow-on you can be a couple of months from full revenue back to almost none." 
His solution: acquiring a company with retainer business — "Acquiring a company that does a lot of execution, but has retainer business will eliminate a lot of the ups and downs we get from a great project."
Reflection question the article poses: "What percentage of your revenue would persist for 90 days without new sales?"
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes

**54.** "The most important thing a founder builds is not services, people, or revenue. It is a firm that no longer depends on them."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

**55.** "Working in the firm improved income. Working on the firm created wealth."
— Collective 54 POV Essay: "The AI Founder"

**56.** Lori Turner-Wilson (Collective 54 member, essay): "The thing that actually sets your company apart is already there — already flashing. You can't see it because you never turn the lights off."
On why firms can't see their own differentiation: "You blink in sync so nobody picks you out of the crowd, and then you sit around wondering why nobody picks you out of the crowd."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes: "The Fireflies and the Floodlights"

**57.** "Bold is risky. A sharp point of view might be the wrong one or tick someone off. That fear is exactly what herds everyone back into the swarm."
— Lori Turner-Wilson, same essay (on why boutique firms commoditise themselves by playing it safe)

**58.** "Your team, as good as they are, doesn't see the work the way you do. They don't have your experience, instincts, or unique ability."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes: "Step Back From the Work. But Don't Lose Sight of It" (on the genuine risk of founders stepping back too completely — there is something that only the founder sees, and the question is how to transfer it, not just how to absent yourself)

## THEME 7: The Transform-Before-or-After Question

Whether to transform the business (add AI, systematise, productise) before selling or whether to sell first — the question nobody is answering cleanly.

**59.** Ujwal Arkalgud (15x EBITDA exit, Collective 54): he treated the profitable services firm as "chapter one rather than the destination" and rebuilt it into a productised, recurring-revenue business before going to market. The uplift was roughly three turns of EBITDA.
The specific moves: converted bespoke consulting to a productised service, built recurring revenue, proved the business runs without founders, pruned unprofitable lines.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.523

**60.** Gavin Bell (Yatter, Facebook ads agency): rebuilt from personal brand to company brand in three years before selling. Four specific moves:
1. Rebranded from his own name to "Yatter"
2. Productised service offerings with named process steps
3. Built a sales team (removed himself from sales)
4. Removed himself from delivery
Then sold to Velstar Group, and cut earn-out from 2 years to 9 months by proving independence from himself as a person.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.529

**61.** Jaryd Krause (buyer perspective): sellers who *haven't* transformed are the opportunity — he actively targets businesses where "manual processes" are visible because he can implement the AI and capture the upside. If you've already done it, his return diminishes.
This creates a strategic choice for founders: transform yourself and get a higher multiple, or sell to someone who wants to transform it and accept a lower multiple.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.548

**62.** Kyle Walbrun (EfficientAide): transformed before selling. The cost was real — lost clients, revenue fell before it rose. But the business model that emerged (human+AI, outcome-based) was fundamentally more sellable because "clients want to delegate outcomes, they don't want to delegate tasks."
— Collective 54 Pod ep.260

**63.** Greg Alexander (sold SBI for $162M, now watches 50 service firm exits via Collective 54): "Identify the new buyer questions around AI and tech enablement" — listed as a specific diligence topic buyers now ask about, not something sellers can ignore.
The implication: even if you don't transform, you will be asked about AI in due diligence. Having no answer costs you.
— Built to Sell Radio ep.492

**64.** The pricing paradox from Collective 54: "Revenue models tied directly to time transform AI efficiency gains into margin compression rather than expansion." Firms that adopt AI without changing their pricing model give the gains away. But changing the pricing model before AI is fully embedded creates client friction.
The founder quoted: "I have spent 13 years seeking an alternative billing model."
— Collective 54 Founder Field Notes: "AI Made the Team Faster..."

## THEME 8: Succession, Legacy, and What They're Actually Protecting

**65.** Darren Smith: his biggest concern was not valuation — it was "100% of the people came with me." He spent 16 years building "the family that I had built" and the measure of a good exit was whether that family survived the transition.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.249

**66.** Brad McAllister: involved managing directors in due diligence months before close specifically so "the buyer could see how good the leadership team was." The message to the buyer: this isn't a founder-dependent business, it's a team-dependent business.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.251

**67.** Marc Weiss: the new CEO caused 40% revenue decline by making changes. His retrospective: "When he started to implement his own strategy, it dropped by 40%." 
The thing that survived Weiss in the business was not documented process — it was the human network. When a new leader broke the network, the value evaporated.
— Collective 54 Pod ep.255

**68.** Succession planning in RIA/advisory firms: traditional 50/50 revenue-split succession models worked when valuations were low. At current 4-6x revenue multiples, those models are "problematic." Firms are now "disaggregating the transaction" — which effectively means founders can't exit cleanly in one step.
— ThinkAdvisor, June 2026: "Succession Planning Is Wealth Management's Defining Talent Crisis"

**69.** Post-exit reframe from a Vistage Chair study: exited founders described being "too experienced to start from scratch, too energised to step away entirely and too seasoned to waste your hard-earned lessons." The thing they actually have is wisdom — and the question after exit is whether wisdom has an outlet.
— Vistage research, June 2026

**70.** Randy Fields (exited founder, became Vistage Chair five years after his exit): described shifting "from success to significance" as the question he couldn't answer until he found a new form of contribution — "working really hard 3 weeks a month" on other people's problems.
— Vistage research, June 2026

## SYNTHESIS: The Six Fears in Their Own Words

Across these sources, 8-figure expertise firm founders express six fears — in roughly this order of frequency and intensity:

**Fear 1 — I will be trapped.** They will sell and then find they have to stay. The earn-out, the rolled equity, the dependency — all described as versions of "I can't leave." The Filosi story (43% of revenue was his personal billings) and Gavin Bell's story (he was the brand) are the canonical examples. The specific fear: selling a business but still owning the job.

**Fear 2 — It's only worth something because of me.** The valuation discount for founder dependence is real and measurable. Ujwal Arkalgud's story (12x to 15x from removing himself) puts numbers on it. But the deeper fear is that the thing they built has no independent existence — it's a reputation, not a business.

**Fear 3 — AI will erase what I know before I can monetise it.** Mike Gibbs and Nick Anderson represent two poles: one is converting knowledge into AI (and protecting it); the other says knowledge is no longer the currency and wisdom is. Both are responding to the same threat. The unnamed advisory firm founder doing hourly billing expresses it most nakedly: AI made my team faster and that is now a threat to my revenue model.

**Fear 4 — I won't know what I am after I sell.** Boris Berenberg's post, Marc Weiss's regret, Adam Rossi's undo wish — all the same thing. The business wasn't just an asset. It was the answer to "who are you?" Jo Swann's clinical observation ("when I sell, then I'll be happy" is the most reliable predictor of a miserable exit) names the mechanism precisely.

**Fear 5 — The wrong partner will destroy what I built.** Darren Smith's concern about his people. Marc Weiss watching revenue fall 40%. Rich Galgano's war with PE that got bad enough he bought his own company back. The specific dread: handing the thing to someone who doesn't understand why it worked.

**Fear 6 — I don't know whether to act now or wait.** The transform-before-or-after question has no clean answer. Transform and you can command 15x. Don't transform and you sell to someone who will do it — but at a discount. Wait and AI erases your moat before you get to market. Act now and you disrupt client relationships mid-revenue-cycle. Multiple founders describe being genuinely stuck on this sequence problem.
