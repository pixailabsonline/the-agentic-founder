The Philosophy

The argument.

Most founders are working on the wrong problem. Here is why the AI era has made that more expensive.

01

What you haven't decided, your company decides for you.

Every AI system optimises for something. If you haven't said what that something is, in precise and specific terms, the system will find its own measure. Usually the closest thing it can count. Not the most important thing. Not the thing that makes your company worth buying or worth passing on.

The company you build is an expression of your decisions, at scale. The ones you made clearly, and the ones you avoided.

02

AI amplifies whatever is already there.

This isn't a warning about technology. It's a structural fact about amplification. An unclear founder becomes faster at being unclear. A company built around its founder's personal relationships builds more personal relationships. A company with clear judgment encoded into how it works compounds that judgment.

The tools improve every quarter. The inputs stay the same until someone changes them.

The founder + AI The result
Unclear → Faster at being unclear
Built around you → More of what buyers won't pay for
Judgment encoded → Company that runs by your rules

03

You can't see your own patterns from inside.

The blind spots in your thinking don't just affect your decisions. They get built into the systems you design. The parts of your company that depend on you personally, the judgments you've never written down, the questions you keep not asking: all of these become the architecture of what you're building.

You can hire people to run departments. You can buy tools to speed up execution. You can't outsource the work of deciding what your company is actually for, and what it should be able to do without you. That requires a mirror.

What it looks like from inside

  • "I need a better strategy"

  • "I can't make this decision"

What's producing it

  • Intent that was never written down
  • A company designed around one person
  • Questions that were never asked
  • Identity constraining the options

Who This Is For

Three situations.

Different problems. The same underlying question: what does this company become, and what part of that decision is yours to make?

01

"I can't step away. The business is me."

Clients trust you personally, not the firm. You're the main salesperson, the best delivery person, and the one who holds the relationships. You know what this means for an exit. You haven't fixed it.

AI has made this more urgent. A company that depends on its founder is harder to sell. It's also harder to run at scale, because the judgment that makes it work hasn't been written down anywhere a system can use it.

02

"I don't know what this company looks like with AI."

Your industry is changing. AI is cutting the cost of work that used to be expensive. You've tried tools. You haven't found the answer to the real question: what is this company actually worth, and what parts of it are defensible?

The answer isn't more AI. It's a clear view of where your company creates value that AI can't replace, and a deliberate choice about what to build around that.

03

"My life's work is still inside my head."

You've built something real. The knowledge, the way of seeing problems, the judgment that makes the work good: it's yours. You haven't found a way to pass it on. AI is now asking what that knowledge is actually worth if it can't outlast you.

The question isn't whether to use AI. It's what of yourself should become part of the company's design, what should become a system, and what should remain personal.
